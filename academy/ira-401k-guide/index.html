Why Traditional Retirement Accounts Underperform

The American retirement system is built on a structural compromise: you get a tax break today in exchange for accepting the restrictions the financial industry has designed into your account. The result is a retirement savings vehicle that is convenient, widely available, and — for most people — chronically underperforming against what is actually possible with the same dollars.

💸

Hidden Fees

Expense ratios, administrative fees, and fund management costs of 1–2% annually compound silently against you every year — often without clear disclosure in plan documents.

🔒

Limited Options

Traditional 401k plans restrict you to a curated menu of mutual funds and target-date funds. No real estate. No private equity. No alternative assets with real income streams.

🎢

Daily Volatility

Stock market downturns can erase years of gains overnight. Retirement portfolios concentrated in equities are exposed to market sentiment — not just economic fundamentals.

After fees consume 1–2% and inflation erodes purchasing power, the frequently cited "average 7–10% S&P 500 return" often shrinks to a real return of 3–5% — and that's assuming you stay invested through every market cycle without panic-selling, which most investors don't. The result is a retirement account that, after decades of contributions, looks significantly smaller in purchasing power terms than the nominal balance suggests.

The question is not whether to use a tax-advantaged retirement account — the tax benefits are genuine and significant. The question is whether you're limited to the investment options your brokerage or employer has pre-selected for you. The answer, which most people don't know, is: you are not. A Self-Directed IRA changes that entirely.

What Is a Self-Directed IRA?

A Self-Directed IRA (SD-IRA) is structurally identical to a traditional or Roth IRA. It follows the same IRS rules. It has the same contribution limits. It has the same tax treatment — pre-tax contributions and tax-deferred growth for Traditional, after-tax contributions and potential tax-free growth for Roth. The difference is entirely in what it allows you to invest in.

"The IRS doesn't care if your IRA holds Apple stock or an apartment complex in Tampa. The rules are the same. Most people just never knew the second option existed."

A standard IRA at a brokerage — Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard — restricts you to publicly traded stocks, bonds, ETFs, and mutual funds. This restriction is not an IRS requirement. It is a custodian restriction. Those brokerages simply don't want to deal with the administrative complexity of holding alternative assets.

A Self-Directed IRA is held at a specialized custodian that is specifically designed to hold alternative assets: real estate, private equity, private lending notes, tax liens, and — critically — interests in private real estate funds like Cardone Capital's Fund 29 and Fund 30. The custodian does not provide investment advice. They hold the assets, process transactions you direct, and file the required IRS forms on your behalf.

How to Roll Over Your 401k Tax-Free

The most common question Cardone Capital's IRA specialists receive is: "Can I roll over my current 401k without paying taxes?" The answer, in most cases, is yes — if you follow the correct procedure.

There are two types of rollovers, and the distinction matters enormously:

Direct Rollover (recommended): Your 401k plan administrator transfers the funds directly to your new SD-IRA custodian — the money never passes through your hands. This is a non-taxable transaction. No withholding. No penalties. No tax event of any kind. This is the standard method and the one Cardone Capital's team guides every investor through.

Indirect Rollover (avoid if possible): The 401k plan sends a check to you personally. You then have 60 days to deposit it into an IRA. The plan is required to withhold 20% for taxes, which means you need to make up that 20% from your own funds to avoid a taxable event. If you miss the 60-day window, the entire amount is treated as a taxable distribution — potentially triggering taxes plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty if you're under 59½.

The Direct Rollover Process — Step by Step

  1. Contact your current 401k plan administrator and request a direct rollover to a self-directed IRA.
  2. Open your new SD-IRA account with your chosen custodian. The custodian will provide rollover instructions and forms.
  3. Your 401k administrator sends the funds directly to the new custodian via wire or check made payable to the custodian FBO (for benefit of) your account.
  4. Funds clear in the new SD-IRA account. You are now ready to direct an investment.

Typical timeline: 5–15 business days for a direct rollover to complete, depending on your plan administrator's processing times. Some employer plans have lock-up requirements — typically 30–90 days after leaving employment — before allowing rollovers. Our team will help you understand the specific requirements for your plan.

Custodian Selection — What to Look For

Choosing the right SD-IRA custodian is one of the most consequential decisions in this process. The custodian holds your retirement assets, processes all investment transactions, and files your IRS forms. A slow, inefficient, or inexperienced custodian can delay your investment, create administrative headaches, and cost you money in fees you didn't anticipate.

01

Experience with private fund investments

Not all SD-IRA custodians are experienced with private equity and real estate fund subscription processes. Ask specifically whether they have processed investments in Regulation D funds before. A custodian unfamiliar with the process can add weeks to your timeline.

02

Clear, published fee schedule

SD-IRA custodians charge account maintenance fees and per-transaction fees. Request the complete fee schedule before opening an account. Annual fees typically range from $100–$500 depending on the custodian and the structure chosen.

03

Fast processing times for wire transfers

When you're ready to invest in Cardone Capital, your custodian needs to process the investment wire quickly. Ask about their average processing time for outgoing investment wires — 2–5 business days is standard; longer is a red flag.

04

Experienced staff who understand your investment

Call them. Ask questions about how they handle a real estate private fund investment. If the representative seems confused by the process, that's important information. A good custodian will be able to walk you through their process confidently.

The Tax Advantages of Real Estate in an IRA

Investing in real estate through a retirement account compounds two powerful sets of tax benefits — the tax treatment of the IRA itself, and the income characteristics of real estate. Understanding how they interact helps you choose the right account structure for your situation.

Traditional SD-IRA advantage: Contributions may be tax-deductible (subject to income limits and whether you have a workplace retirement plan). Your real estate distributions flow into the IRA and compound tax-deferred. When you eventually take distributions in retirement, they are taxed as ordinary income — but potentially at a lower rate than your current working income.

Roth SD-IRA advantage: Contributions are made after tax, but all growth and qualified distributions are potentially tax-free. A Roth SD-IRA that accumulates real estate income over 20 years — completely tax-free — is one of the most powerful wealth-building structures available under US tax law. If you believe real estate values and rents will be significantly higher 20 years from now, the Roth structure captures all of that appreciation without a future tax event.

A critical note on UBTI: Real estate held inside an IRA can generate Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI) if the property is leveraged with debt. Cardone Capital's IR team and your tax advisor can help you understand whether UBTI applies to your specific situation. For many investors, the benefits of the IRA structure outweigh the UBTI consideration — but it's an important factor to review before investing.

This article is educational, not tax advice. Always consult a qualified tax advisor before making any rollover or SD-IRA investment decisions.
